Project Overview
With user interactions, there is a critical pain point: users often shop under pressure, whether it’s during a busy workday, while managing family responsibilities, or in situations requiring quick decisions. Many existing processes involve too many steps, leading to frustration, abandoned carts, and missed opportunities for engagement.

Working with a team of 3, our goal was to design a more intuitive experience that aligned with the fast-paced, convenience-driven needs of Doordash’s app users. By reducing the number of clicks and streamlining key workflows—such as finding commonly purchased items, managing lists, and completing checkout—we aimed to minimize cognitive load and make the process as seamless as possible. This project aimed to not only enhanced user satisfaction but also align with DoorDash’s mission to provide convenience, ultimately boosting customer retention and encouraging greater app usage for grocery shopping.

Search Opportunity
Before choosing Doordash as a candidate for an AUI, my team and I heavily researched the market - diving into different industries to determine how to best incorporate AUI’s into user interactions. We brainstormed 10 different ideations and underwent a process of elimination, below are a few AUI ideas my team and I discovered:
AUI in Subway App for Drink/Dessert in Cart
  • Scenario: User is buying a sandwich from the Subway app
  • Flow up to trigger: User is adding items to cart
  • Inference the system makes: The user needs to add an item they buy regularly with their sandwich
  • Computational action based on inference: Automatically using past data to suggest a drink or dessert that the user would want with a sandwich
  • Frequency of users encountering this AUI: Every time a user is going to order just a sandwich and not add-on a meal & dessert
  • Availability of labeled data: High, we know what the customer is ordering and if they want to have a particular dessert or a particular drink
  • Impact of errors: Low, the customer will not take the suggested item
  • Co-creation of value: The user on the cart page will not need to go back to find an item they like in the app. For Subway, they add value by directly suggesting and selling add-ons to customers who are only buying a sandwich.
Canvas (LMS)
  • Scenario: Student is checking assignments/grades on Canvas app
  • Flow up to trigger: User logs into canvas
  • Inference the system makes: Which section does the user most frequently visit in course on canvas
  • Computational action based on inference: Automatically using past data to suggest a drink or dessert that the user would want with a sandwich
  • Frequency of users encountering this AUI: Everytime a user is going to a particular section on canvas repeatedly
  • Availability of labeled data: High, we know what course the user is seeing and the section in the course which is labeled
  • Impact of errors: Low, the customer will not use the shortcut
  • Co-creation of value: The user saves time and effort to go to the section they want to visit frequently. Canvas can benefit from this by adding it as a feature that they charge for in an enterprise sale or sale to an institute.
X-Auto Suggest Tags
  • Scenario: When users post a tweet, they often spend a lot of time finding the right tags
  • Flow up to trigger: The user opens the tweet composition page on Twitter
  • Inference the system makes: Analyzes the user's previous tweet history and current topic/content, then presents the most frequently used tags based on the user's past tweet history and tags that users usually use together.
  • Computational action based on inference: Machine learning models analyzes the user's behavior and predict the most suitable tags
  • Frequency of users encountering this AUI: Every time they open the tweet composition page to post a new tweet
  • Availability of labeled data: Requires access to and analysis of the user's tweet history as well as publicly available tag usage data
  • Impact of errors:The recommended tags may be irrelevant, leading to user dissatisfaction (False positives). The system may miss some relevant tags, thus decreasing the chance of getting higher views (False negatives)
  • Co-creation of value: Help users increase the visibility and impact of their tweets. For X, it attracts creators and influencers to stay on X, therefore more people would use X and more companies would pay to advertise on X
DoorDash One-Click Order
  • Scenario: If users want to order food that they have ordered multiple times, they need to go to their order history to find the order and click reorder.
  • Flow up to trigger: The user navigates to the Doordash homepage.
  • Inference the system makes: Analyzes DoorDash ordering history to find often-purchased items, then suggests 2-3 frequently ordered food items to display at the top of the homepage with a button to order with one-click.
  • Computational action based on inference: Algorithms to identify the most frequently ordered food items for each user.
  • Frequency of users encountering this AUI: Every time they visit the Doordash homepage to order their favorite food
  • Availability of labeled data: Requires access to and analysis of the user's order history data on Doordash to determine frequently ordered food items.
  • Impact of errors:The system may suggest irrelevant ordered food items,leading to user frustration (False positives). The system may miss some relevant frequently ordered food items, reducing the usefulness of the feature (False negatives).
  • Co-creation of value: For users, it saves time and effort in placing orders. For Doordash, it increases the likelihood of repeat orders and impulse purchases, boosting revenue.
Solution Conceptual
From our overall ideation, we had several viable ideas that we were planning to implement. Initially, our team decided on implementing a Walmart AUI where customers quick add frequently purchased items into the cart with a single click. However, after the critique, our team decided to pivot to DoorDash as there was more opportunity to create value. We realized that our Walmart AUI didn’t generate sufficient revenue as it we did not have a specific target audience that would use the AUI. However, we were able to identify that DoorDash’s current add to cart selection is time consuming and extremely repetitive for existing users. In addition, this also applies for frequent physical grocery customers as driving/going to the store and individually finding each item for shoppers who buy in bulk.
Therefore, our team saw a valuable opportunity for DoorDash to bring physical grocery shoppers to the DoorDash app and retain existing customers by improving the efficiency of the UI through a Single-Click “Add to Cart” AUI.

Wireframing Process
Low Fidelity
For the concept of arranging stores by purchase frequency, we've opted to include a badge on the Grocery page labeled "Sorted for You." This informs users that the list is arranged based on their buying frequency.
In the popup showcasing users' most frequent purchases, we've employed a prominent heading stating, "Suggestions for you from ..." to emphasize personalization. Each item is displayed with its name, price, quantity, and options for adjusting quantity or removing the item, allowing for easy customization. The system records these changes to refine future suggestions. To draw attention, we've highlighted the "Add All Items to Cart" button in a darker color.
Upon selecting "Add All Items to Cart," users are directed to the checkout page with a list of items from the popup, streamlining the checkout process for added efficiency.
Mid Fidelity
For the mid-fi wireframes, we opted to integrate DoorDash's current color scheme to ensure consistency with the app's aesthetic. This involved utilizing the DoorDash red color for the badge and "Add All Items to Cart" button.
Additionally, we included images, names, prices, and quantities for each item to make the wireframes look more realistic.
High Fidelity
After receiving feedback from critique, we realized the necessity of expanding the item list to better showcase the significant time-saving benefits of the AUI in selecting frequently purchased groceries. Additionally, we identified the need to include the total price and item count in the popup. This will allow users to quickly assess the total cost of their selections and the number of items chosen without navigating to another page, streamlining the shopping process and reducing friction.
As a result, for the hi-fi wireframes, we increased the item count in the popup to 23. To indicate the length of the list, we introduced a scrollbar and bordered the item list to distinguish it from other content within the popup.
Furthermore, we included a bold line of text below the item list to display the total price and item count. We ensured alignment between the items listed in the checkout page and the items in the popup.
ROI Pitch
For our draft pitch, we organized the slides into:
  • Comparison between the current UI and our new AUI
  • Value flow model
  • Best customer & scenario
  • Technical feasibility
  • Financial viability
  • User acceptance
We began the pitch by showcasing a flowchart of DoorDash's current UI for the grocery section, illustrating how users navigate various screens and undertake repetitive steps to add frequently purchased items to their cart.
Following this overview of user behavior patterns, we presented two concepts for implementing the AUI: an ordered store list and a popup featuring frequently ordered items. We then introduced our streamlined 3-step AUI, designed to facilitate quick and easy checkout for users' frequent purchases.
Following the comparison, we presented a value flow model illustrating the exchange of value among stores, app users, DoorDash, and delivery drivers.
Subsequently, we introduced our ideal customer and scenario: targeting users who purchase groceries both in-store and through DoorDash, the AUI aims to transition these users to primarily online grocery shopping by offering reduced friction and enhanced convenience.

Reflection
As I went through this project, I had no prior knowledge of what an AUI was, but I found it to be an extremely interesting as modern technology and processes are becoming more automated and efficient. As a team, we had several different ideas and seeing how we decided to find the pros and cons of each option to find the most viable product was something that helped me further my collaboration and communication skills.
In addition, I learned how to implement a product feature from the beginning to the end. Even if it was just an AUI, these small interfaces are what can heavily affect a consumer’s choices, generating more revenue for companies. Therefore, finding a very simple yet effective solution to product problem is something that I also was able to learn more about.
Overall, I was able to understand not only how to work with a product team but also the ins and outs of fully developing and launching features in products and how to make it appealing towards different types of consumers and users.
